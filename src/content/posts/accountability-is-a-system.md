<section data-title="Thesis">
  <h2>You can't exhort your way to ownership</h2>
  <p>When ownership is weak, the instinct is to address it with words: an all-hands about accountability, a values slide, a stern reminder that we all need to step up. It feels like action. It changes nothing.</p>
  <p>Accountability is a property of structure, not sentiment. People act accountable when the system makes ownership clear, visible, and unavoidable — and they diffuse responsibility when it doesn't, no matter how inspiring the speech.</p>
  <blockquote>Accountability isn't who you blame afterward. It's who decided before.</blockquote>
</section>

<section data-title="Pattern">
  <h2>The talk that changes nothing</h2>
  <p>The pattern repeats across every struggling org I've seen. A leader notices things falling through cracks, delivers a heartfelt message about ownership, and watches the same gaps reappear the following month. The message wasn't wrong. It was aimed at the wrong layer.</p>
  <p>Behavior followed the structure, and the structure still routed every real decision back to ambiguity.</p>
</section>

<section data-title="Mechanism">
  <h2>Why structure beats sentiment</h2>
  <p>People optimize for the incentives and clarity in front of them. If two teams could each own a problem, neither will — not from malice, but because shared ownership is no ownership. If decisions have no named owner, they wait for the most senior person in the room, and that person becomes the bottleneck for everything.</p>
  <p>The lever is not motivation. It's removing the ambiguity that lets responsibility slide.</p>
</section>

<section data-title="Control">
  <h2>The operating model that holds</h2>
  <ul>
    <li><strong>One directly responsible owner per outcome.</strong> Named, known, and singular. Co-ownership is a euphemism for unowned.</li>
    <li><strong>Decisions in writing, with the owner attached.</strong> The record is what makes accountability survive turnover and time.</li>
    <li><strong>A standing operating rhythm</strong> where owners report on their commitments to peers, not just to managers. Visibility to the room is what makes it real.</li>
  </ul>
</section>

<section data-title="Final Take">
  <h2>The final take</h2>
  <p>Design the system and the behavior follows. Name the owners, write the decisions down, and make commitments visible to people whose respect matters. Do that and you'll never need the accountability speech — the structure will be saying it for you, every day, without a single slide.</p>
</section>
